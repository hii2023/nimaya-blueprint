01
Mission Understanding
Why this program exists and why it matters now

Start by showing you understand the "why" before the "what." The goal is not just to teach ChatGPT — it is to make sure young women from low-income backgrounds are not left behind when AI transforms every job category.

  • The problem in one line: 65% of entry-level jobs will be reshaped by AI by 2030. Women from underserved communities have the least access to AI skills, making them the most at risk.
  • Nimaya's response: A free, 40-hour, hands-on program that does not just teach AI tools but builds real workplace confidence alongside technical ability.
  • Scale already proven: 10,000+ Naaris across 5 states. Partners include IndiaAI (Government of India's Yuva AI Mission), Escorts Kubota (6,000+ Naaris), and Datamatics Business Solutions.
  • Ashoka University connection: The Centre for Social and Behavioral Change (CSBC) is co-designing this expansion — meaning you have academic rigor and research backing, not just a training curriculum.
What to write in your report

Mention that Nimaya sits at the intersection of employability and AI readiness. Quote the partnership with IndiaAI as proof of national-level credibility. Then say: "Expanding to Ahmedabad makes strategic sense because Gujarat is India's industrial backbone — AI-ready women here will immediately enter a job market that needs them."

02
City Context — Ahmedabad
Who lives here, where they are, and why this city is ready

Ahmedabad has a population of 8+ million. Around 20% live in approximately 710 slum pockets — mostly concentrated in the eastern and northern industrial corridors. These areas have a large population of 18–25 year old women who have completed school but have not found stable work.

  • Key underserved zones to focus on: Bapunagar, Vatwa, Naroda, Odhav (east and north industrial belt). These areas have dense youth populations near existing NGO networks and ITI colleges.
  • Why these areas: SAATH Charitable Trust has already been working in Ahmedabad slums since 1989 — their Women@Work program targets exactly the 18–25 age group from slum communities. Their network can be a pipeline, not a competitor.
  • Language reality: Most target learners are Gujarati-speaking. Some Hindi. English is limited. The training materials need to be delivered in Gujarati/Hindi with English vocabulary introduced gradually. This is an advantage Altaf has over any outsider program manager.
  • Family dynamics: In many households, the parents or husband need to give permission for a girl to attend training. Programs that run 9am–1pm (not evenings) and are based at a college (a trusted institution) see lower dropout. Keep this in mind when picking venues.
Example you can use in the report

"A 21-year-old woman in Bapunagar who completed her B.Com but cannot find work is the exact Naari we are designing for. She is smart, motivated, and living 3 km from Government Polytechnic for Girls on Ahmedabad-380015 — which is close enough to attend a morning batch without overnight travel."

03
90-Day Program Setup Plan
Three phases with real milestones

Break the first 90 days into three phases, each with a different focus. Think of it as: Phase 1 = build the foundation, Phase 2 = run and learn, Phase 3 = document and scale.

  • Phase 1 (Days 1–30): Sign MoUs with 3 colleges, hire a local assistant coordinator, lock venue, build enrollment pipeline. Deliverable: 60 enrolled candidates ready for screening.
  • Phase 2 (Days 31–60): Select 40 Naaris, run orientation, deliver Batch 1 (32 offline hours). Deliverable: 40 Naaris completing 80%+ attendance with mid-batch assessment done.
  • Phase 3 (Days 61–90): Complete Batch 1 (remaining 8 online hours), certification, placement/outcome tracking begins. Deliverable: Batch 1 impact report ready, Batch 2 enrollment open.
04
Partnership Strategy
Who to approach, in what order, and how

Partnerships are how you get learners (colleges and NGOs), funding (corporates), and credibility (government bodies). You need at least one from each tier before Batch 1 starts.

  • Tier 1 — Learner Pipeline (Colleges/ITIs): Government Polytechnic for Girls, Ahmedabad (Nr. Atira, 380015), F.D. Arts & Commerce College for Women, M.P. Arts and M.H. Commerce College for Women. These are large women-only institutions with placement cells and placement officers you can approach directly.
  • Tier 2 — NGO Co-partners: SAATH Charitable Trust (saath.org) already runs Women@Work for 18–25 year olds from slums — exact same demographic. Manav Kartavya (Satellite area) does livelihood training. These orgs already have community trust; Nimaya's AI component is something they don't offer.
  • Tier 3 — Corporate CSR: Adani Foundation runs women skill development through ASDC centers across Gujarat. Torrent Pharmaceuticals and Zydus Cadila both have strong CSR in Ahmedabad. Approach them for venue sponsorship, mentor access, or live project briefs (not cash first).
  • Tier 4 — Government: Gujarat CSR Authority (gcsra.org) is a Government of Gujarat initiative that connects NGOs to corporate CSR. Register with them. Also approach the District Employment Exchange, Ahmedabad for co-branding the program.
How to approach a college (step by step)

Step 1: Email the Principal with a one-page overview. Step 2: Request a 20-minute meeting. Step 3: Bring a printed program brochure + 1 alumni story from an existing Nimaya batch. Step 4: Propose that Nimaya provides the training for free and the college provides a room and promotes it to students. Step 5: Sign a simple 1-page MoU within 2 weeks.

05
Learner Selection Framework
Who gets in, how you pick them, and how you keep them

The selection process is critical — too easy and you fill seats but lose quality. Too strict and you exclude the exact women who need the program most. Aim for motivated over qualified.

  • Eligibility: Women aged 18–27, completed Class 10 minimum, based in or near Ahmedabad, not currently in full-time employment (or employed part-time and willing to attend morning batches). No prior tech knowledge needed.
  • Selection in 3 steps: (1) Application form — basic info + "why do you want to join" in Gujarati. (2) Group information session — 30-minute session where you explain what the program involves. Anyone who shows up is already motivated. (3) Brief 1:1 conversation — not an interview, just to understand their current situation and flag dropout risk early.
  • Dropout risk signals to watch for: Lives more than 8km away without personal transport, has a child below 3 years old without a care option, family member has expressed disapproval. These don't disqualify — they tell you who needs extra support.
  • Retention tools: Peer buddy system (pair each new Naari with someone from the same area), WhatsApp group for the batch with daily engagement, mid-batch celebration (a small event at week 3 to mark progress).
Real example of dropout prevention

SAATH's Udaan program (which has placed 40,000+ youth from Ahmedabad slums) found that peer accountability groups cut dropout by 30%. Nimaya can replicate this by assigning a "batch buddy" on Day 1 and making buddy check-ins part of the daily routine.

06
Program Delivery Oversight
What happens in the room, and how you ensure quality

The 40-hour curriculum covers: building chatbots, agentic AIs, AI-powered research, communication tactics, creative problem-solving, presentation skills, and real-world projects. Your job is not to teach — it is to make sure the trainer teaches it well, and the Naaris are actually learning.

  • Trainer profile needed: Someone with hands-on experience with tools like ChatGPT, Gemini, Canva AI, Notion AI. Does not need to be an AI engineer — a working professional who uses AI daily is better than a theoretical teacher. Budget: ₹1,500–2,500 per training hour in Ahmedabad.
  • Trainer sourcing options in Ahmedabad: LinkedIn search "AI trainer Ahmedabad", post on IIMA alumni groups, check freelance platforms like Toptal or Instahyre. Also check if CEPT University or Nirma University faculty have relevant profiles.
  • Quality checks you run: (1) Attend first session of every new trainer to observe. (2) Run a 5-question quick quiz at the end of each week — not for grades, just to spot if concepts landed. (3) Collect written feedback from Naaris after every module (simple smiley scale + one text field).
  • Live project component: Partner with a local business or startup to give Naaris a real brief. Example: A local kirana chain wants to know "how can we use AI for inventory reminders?" Naaris solve it in teams. This is the most powerful part of the program — real work builds real confidence.
  • Mentor matching: Ask your corporate CSR partners (Adani, Torrent) to nominate 2–3 mid-level employees as mentors for the batch. One mentor per 10 Naaris. Schedule a 45-minute video call at week 3 and week 6.
07
Impact Tracking and Reporting
What you measure, how, and for whom

Impact tracking serves two purposes: (1) it tells you if the program is working, and (2) it gives Nimaya's central team and donors the proof they need to justify continued investment. Both matter equally.

  • Quantitative metrics: Enrollment vs completion rate (target 80%+), attendance per session, pre/post assessment score improvement, number of AI tasks completed in final project, placement or internship conversion within 90 days of batch end.
  • Qualitative metrics: Confidence self-assessment (simple 1–5 scale on "I can use AI at work" measured at start and end), trainer observation notes, 3 Naari stories per batch for donor reports.
  • Your Google Sheets master tracker columns: Name, Age, Area (Bapunagar/Vatwa etc.), Education, College Partner, Attendance (Day 1–10), Quiz Score W1/W2/W3/W4, Confidence Pre/Post, Live Project Grade, Outcome (Placed/Internship/Continuing/Dropped).
  • Reporting cadence: Weekly 15-min sync with central team (share tracker link, flag issues). Monthly 1-page PDF report to partner institutions. Quarterly impact snapshot for CSR partners with photos, quotes, and numbers.
One metric that impresses donors

Track "AI tool usage 30 days after program" — send a simple WhatsApp poll to all Batch 1 graduates 30 days later: "Did you use AI at work/study this month?" Yes/No + one example. This shows lasting impact, not just attendance.

08
Risk Register
What can go wrong and what you will do about it

The fact that you can name risks before they happen is what separates a program manager from a program executor. Include this section to show maturity.

  • Low enrollment: College partner pulls out or does not promote the program. Mitigation: Never rely on a single partner. Have 3 MoUs before Batch 1. If 1 pulls out, 2 remain.
  • Trainer unavailability: Your main trainer gets sick or takes another offer mid-batch. Mitigation: Always onboard 1 backup trainer. Conduct a shadow session with the backup in Week 1.
  • High dropout after Week 2: The most common dropout window in skill programs is Week 2–3. Mitigation: Intervene at first absence. Direct call from you (not WhatsApp) to the Naari or her family member within 24 hours of absence.
  • Venue conflict: College decides to use the training room for exams. Mitigation: Always have a backup venue identified (e.g., a nearby community hall or NGO office). SAATH's Ahmedabad office or a Regus conference room at ₹5,000–8,000/day as last resort.
  • Partner disengagement: A corporate CSR partner who promised mentors or a live project brief goes quiet. Mitigation: All commitments in writing before the batch starts. Follow up monthly — do not wait for them to disappear.
09
Budget Overview
Realistic cost estimate per batch of 40 Naaris

Show you understand costs without overspending. The goal is to run a quality program at a realistic budget that can be funded by 1–2 CSR partners per batch.

  • Venue (32 offline hours over 16 sessions x 2hrs): If through college MoU — free. If rented: ₹5,000–8,000/day x 8 days = ₹40,000–64,000. Aim for college MoU to keep this at zero.
  • Trainer fees: ₹2,000/hr x 40 hrs = ₹80,000 per batch. Negotiate a lump-sum ₹75,000 for the full batch engagement.
  • Training materials: Printed workbooks, pens, A4 paper, markers — ₹200/student x 40 = ₹8,000.
  • Devices: If college provides laptops/desktops — free. If not, consider requesting 10 laptops from a CSR partner on loan. Do not buy unless budget exists.
  • Coordinator cost (part-time local hire): ₹15,000/month x 3 months = ₹45,000 per batch cycle.
  • Travel and field ops: Auto, Metro, petrol for Altaf's outreach and oversight — ₹5,000/month x 3 = ₹15,000.
  • Contingency (10%): ₹20,000–25,000.
Total per batch (best case vs realistic)

Best case (college venue free, devices borrowed): ₹1.7L–2L per batch. Realistic (venue rented + devices rented): ₹3L–3.5L per batch. Scale to 5 batches/year = ₹12L–17L annual Ahmedabad budget — well within the range of a single mid-size CSR grant from Adani Foundation or Torrent.

No Existing Field Network in Slum Communities

Professional network is corporate — US Healthcare, data accounts, product teams. That network does not overlap with the communities we want to reach in Bapunagar or Vatwa. Community trust in underserved areas is earned slowly, not imported from a LinkedIn profile.

How I will close this gap

Start through institutions already trusted by the community — SAATH, Manav Kartavya, Government Polytechnic for Girls. Let credible intermediaries introduce the program. Attend community events (college orientation days, local NGO gatherings) before formally pitching. Trust the institution to build trust with the community first.

No Formal EdTech or Curriculum Background

Evaluating whether a 40-hour AI curriculum is actually building skills — versus just filling time — requires pedagogical instincts that come from experience in learning design. Knowing if a trainer is good, not just likeable, is a different skill than knowing if a client account is healthy.

How I will close this gap

Use Nimaya's existing quality rubrics and Ashoka CSBC frameworks — I don't need to invent assessment methods, I need to implement them well. Attend 2 full sessions at an existing Nimaya batch city before running Ahmedabad independently. Focus on data: pre/post assessment scores tell me if learning happened, not my gut feeling about the trainer.

Corporate Clarity vs. Field Ambiguity

15 years in an environment with defined SLAs, escalation paths, and process documentation. Field program management means a venue cancels the morning of a session, a trainer goes silent on WhatsApp, or a Naari stops attending without explanation. There is no ticket system for this.

How I will close this gap

Build minimum viable structures early and iterate weekly. Keep a "blockers and assumptions" log — flag ambiguity as a data point, not a failure. Reframe: ambiguity is what makes this role valuable for someone with a structured background. The ability to bring structure to chaos is exactly what ground-level programs lack most.

Learner Dropout is Emotionally Complex

When a Naari stops attending, the reason is usually not laziness — it is family pressure, a sick parent, financial stress, or feeling out of place in a room full of people who seem more confident. Managing dropout requires empathy first and operations second. No prior experience with this kind of human complexity at scale.

How I will close this gap

Proactively address barriers at the enrollment stage — identify at-risk Naaris in the selection conversation, not after they drop. Build peer buddy accountability into Day 1. Learn from SAATH's Udaan program: they cut dropout by 30% using peer groups in Ahmedabad's slum communities. Call on first absence — not WhatsApp. A personal call within 24 hours of the first miss is the single most effective retention intervention known in field programs.
